Mixtures and Separation Techniques
Mixtures

- Unlike in compounds, there are no chemical bonds between substances in a mixture.

- A mixture is made up of elements and/or compounds, and can be separated using physical methods.

- The properties of a mixture are a combination of the properties of the components in the mixture.









Separation Techniques
Filtration

- Filtration is used to separate insoluble solids from liquids (e.g. separating sand and water)

- It involves pouring the mixture through a cone lined with filter paper.

- The liquid and any dissolved substances will pass through the filter paper.

- Insoluble solids will not fit through the tiny holes in the filter paper, and will collect on the paper instead.





Evaporation

- Evaporation is used to separate soluble solids from liquids (e.g. separating salt and water), but only if the solid doesn't decompose when heated.

- To separate them, pour the solution into an evaporating dish.

- Slowly heat the solution. The solvent will start to evaporate and the solution will get more concentrated.

- Eventually, crystals will begin to form. Keep heating until all the solvent has evaporated and you are left with dry crystals.





Crystallisation

- Crystallisation is also used to separate soluble solids from liquids, particularly if the solid decomposes when heated.

- To separate them, pour the solution into an evaporating dish.

- Gently heat the solution. The solvent will start to evaporate and the solution will get more concentrated.

- Once you see crystals starting to form, remove the dish from the heat and leave to cool.

- More crystals will begin to form as the solute becomes insoluble in the cold, highly concentrated solution.

- Either filter out the crystals or allow them to dry.
