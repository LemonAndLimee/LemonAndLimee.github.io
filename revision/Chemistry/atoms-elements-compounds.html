Atoms, Elements and Compounds
Atoms
The Nucleus

- The nucleus is in the centre of the atom.

- It contains protons (+) and neutrons (no charge).

- It's positively charged because of the protons.


Protons vs Electrons

- Protons are positively charged, and electrons are negatively charged.

- The number of protons and electrons in an atom are the same.

- As a result, atoms have no overall charge.





Atomic Number and Mass Number

- The atomic number tells you how many protons there are.

- The mass number tells you the total number of protons and neutrons in the atom.

- To work out the number of neutrons, subtract the atomic number from the mass number.









Elements

- Elements are made up of atoms with the same atomic number.

- The number of protons in an atom determine what element it is.

- There are over 100 different elements.


- Elements can be represented by one or two letter symbols.

- For example, Oxygen = O, Carbon = C, Iron = Fe

- These symbols are used to write out chemical equations.





Isotopes

- Isotopes are different forms of the same element.

- They have the same number of protons and electrons but a different number of neutrons.

- This means isotopes have the same atomic number but a different mass number.

- As many different isotopes of elements exist, relative atomic mass is used instead of the mass number when referring to the whole element.

- Relative atomic mass is an average that takes into account the different masses and abundances of the different isotopes of that element.









Compounds

- When elements react with each other, they can form compounds.

- These are substances made from two or more elements, held together by chemical bonds.

- Making bonds involves gaining, losing or sharing electrons - the nucleus isn't affected.

- A chemical reaction is often required to break these bonds.



- Compounds can be represented using symbols.

- For example, Carbon Dioxide = CO2, Sodium Chloride = NaCl, Sulfuric Acid = H2SO4
