The invention of the microscope was thought to have occurred in the Netherlands in 1590 by makers of eye glasses. English scientist Robert Hooke published a book in 1665, featuring drawings of close-up images, such as the eye of a fly. Since then microscopes have been developed, and come in two forms - the light microscope and the electron microscope.



Electron Microscopes

Electron microscopes use electrons instead of light to make an image. Electron wavelength can be up to 100, 000 times smaller than that of visible light - this means they can take images of higher magnification. The first electron microscope was made by two German scientists in 1931, and was a transmission electron microscope (TEM). In 1937, the scanning electron microscope was invented (SEM).

Transmission electron microscopes fire a large beam of electrons through a very thin slice of the specimen, all at the same time. The image is formed from the electrons that pass through the specimen. The images formed are flat cross-sections, particularly useful for looking at cells.

Scanning electron microscopes have a smaller beam of electrons that scans across the whole image, but not all at the same time. They scatter across to make the image, and the image is more 3-dimensional.

Electron microscopes can magnify more than light microscopes and have a greater resolution. The resolution is the shortest distance between two parts of the specimen that can be distinctly seen. With a light microscope the maximum resolution is 200 nanometres. With an electron microscope the maximum resolution is 0.1 nanometres.
